Simple Pendulum — Determine g

Aim: To investigate how the period of a simple pendulum varies with its length, and to determine the acceleration due to gravity g from a T² vs L graph.

Apparatus

Retort stand + clamp String (≈ 1 m) Small dense bob (metal) Metre rule Stopwatch (±0.01 s) Protractor Set square / fiducial marker

Theory

Period of a Simple Pendulum T = 2π √(L / g)

Squaring both sides:

Linearised Form T² = (4π² / g) × L

Plotting T² (y-axis) against L (x-axis) gives a straight line through the origin. The gradient = 4π²/g, so:

Determining g g = 4π² / gradient
💡 Key Requirements:
• Angle of swing must be small (< 10°) for the formula to be valid.
• Measure length from pivot to centre of bob (not bottom of bob).
• Time at least 20 complete oscillations and divide by 20 to reduce timing error.

Procedure

1
Set up the pendulum. Measure length L from the clamp to the centre of the bob using the metre rule.
2
Displace the bob to a small angle (< 10°) and release it without pushing.
3
Use a fiducial marker at the rest position. Start timing as the bob passes the marker. Count 20 complete oscillations.
4
Record time for 20 oscillations (t₂₀). Calculate period: T = t₂₀ / 20.
5
Repeat for at least 6 different lengths (e.g. 30, 40, 50, 60, 70, 80 cm).
6
Plot T² vs L. Draw a best-fit line. Calculate gradient → find g.
💡 Why 20 oscillations? Human reaction time is about ±0.2 s. If T ≈ 1.5 s, the error in one period = 0.2/1.5 ≈ 13%. Over 20 oscillations: error = 0.2/30 ≈ 0.7%. Much better!

⚠️ Sources of Error

  • Reaction time error: Starting/stopping the stopwatch is delayed by ~0.2 s. Reduced by timing 20+ oscillations.
  • Counting error: Miscounting oscillations. Use a fiducial marker and count carefully from zero.
  • Length measurement: Difficult to measure exactly to the centre of the bob. Use a set square to locate the centre. Measure with bob at rest.
  • Large amplitude: If the angle > 10°, the T = 2π√(L/g) formula is no longer accurate. The period increases with amplitude. Keep angle < 10°.
  • Air resistance: Causes the amplitude to decrease over time, slightly affecting timing. Use a small, dense (heavy) bob to minimise drag.
  • Non-rigid string: String may stretch under tension, changing effective length. Use inextensible string.
  • Parallax error: When reading the ruler. Place ruler close to the string; read at eye level.
  • Oscillation not in single plane: Bob swings in an ellipse instead of a straight line. Release carefully without sideways push.

✅ Precautions

  • Time from the equilibrium position (where the bob moves fastest) — easier to identify the exact moment of crossing.
  • Allow the pendulum to swing 2–3 times before starting the stopwatch to establish a steady rhythm.
  • Repeat timing and take the average of at least 2 readings for each length.
  • Plot the graph as you go — an anomalous point is easier to spot and re-measure.

🏆 Extension Challenges

  • Does the mass of the bob affect the period? Test with different bobs of the same length.
  • What happens when the angle exceeds 15°? 30°? Measure and compare with the formula.
  • Try to achieve g = 9.81 ± 0.2 m/s². What's the smallest percentage error you can get?

Spring-Mass Oscillations — Period vs Mass

Aim: To investigate how the period of vertical oscillation of a spring-mass system depends on the attached mass, and to determine the spring constant k.

Apparatus

Helical spring Retort stand + clamp Slotted masses (50–400 g) Mass hanger Stopwatch Ruler Fiducial marker

Theory

Period of a Spring-Mass System T = 2π √(m / k)
Linearised Form T² = (4π² / k) × m    →    k = 4π² / gradient

Procedure

1
Hang the spring from the clamp. Attach a known mass and let it reach equilibrium.
2
Pull the mass down by 2–3 cm (small amplitude) and release. Time 20 oscillations.
3
Record t₂₀ and calculate T = t₂₀ / 20.
4
Repeat for at least 6 different masses.
5
Plot T² vs m. Gradient = 4π²/k → find k.

⚠️ Sources of Error

  • Reaction time: Same as pendulum — time 20 oscillations to minimise.
  • Lateral sway: Mass may swing sideways instead of bouncing purely vertically. Guide the mass to oscillate vertically only.
  • Exceeding elastic limit: If too much mass causes permanent deformation of the spring. Check spring returns to original length after removing mass.
  • Large amplitude: Very large displacements may make the oscillation non-sinusoidal. Keep amplitude small (2–3 cm).
  • Mass of spring: The formula assumes the spring is massless. A heavy spring adds to effective oscillating mass, increasing T. Use a light spring with heavy bobs.
  • Resonance with stand: The retort stand may sway in sympathy. Clamp the stand firmly or weight the base.
  • Counting error: Same as pendulum — use a fiducial marker at the equilibrium position.

✅ Precautions

  • Wait for transient vibrations to die down before starting timing.
  • Count from the equilibrium position where the mass moves fastest.
  • Repeat timing and take averages.
  • Do not exceed the elastic limit of the spring.

Spring Balance — Calibration & Measurement

Aim: To calibrate a spring as a force meter using known weights, then use it to measure unknown forces.

Apparatus

Helical spring Retort stand + clamp Ruler Known masses (50 g to 400 g) Unknown masses (2–3) Card strip for marking

Procedure

1
Calibration: Record the pointer position with no load (0 N mark).
2
Add known masses one at a time (e.g. 100 g = 1.0 N). Mark the pointer position on the card for each.
3
Continue until you have marks for 0 N, 1.0 N, 2.0 N, 3.0 N, 4.0 N (or as many as the spring allows).
4
Measurement: Hang an unknown mass. Read the force from your calibrated scale.
5
Check by weighing the unknown mass on a balance and calculating W = mg.
Add known masses and mark the scale to calibrate your spring balance.

⚠️ Sources of Error

  • Zero error: The pointer may not return exactly to the zero mark after removing loads. Check and adjust the zero before each measurement.
  • Parallax error: Reading the pointer position from an angle. Read at eye level perpendicular to the scale.
  • Hysteresis: The spring may give slightly different readings when loading vs unloading. Always load masses (not unload) for calibration.
  • Exceeding elastic limit: Too much mass permanently stretches the spring, ruining calibration. Do not exceed the rated capacity.
  • Spring fatigue: Repeated use may change the spring constant over time. Re-calibrate periodically.
  • Temperature effects: Metal springs expand/contract with temperature. Calibrate and measure at the same temperature.
  • Oscillation: Spring may bounce when mass is added. Wait for equilibrium before reading.
  • Interpolation error: Reading between calibration marks introduces uncertainty. Mark at small intervals (e.g. every 0.5 N).

🏆 Challenge

  • How close can you get to the actual weight of the unknown objects?
  • What is the resolution (smallest readable division) of your calibrated scale?
  • Compare with a commercial spring balance — what is the percentage difference?

📊 Comprehensive Error Analysis Guide

Understanding errors is essential for O-Level practical exams. This guide covers everything you need to know about identifying, classifying and reducing errors in experiments.

🔍 Types of Errors

🎯 Systematic Errors

Errors that shift all readings in the same direction by the same amount. They affect accuracy but not precision.

  • Zero error — instrument doesn't read zero when it should
  • Wrongly calibrated instrument — all readings consistently too high or too low
  • Background reading — e.g. background radiation not accounted for
  • Parallax (if consistently reading from the same wrong angle)

Reduced by: Recalibrating instruments, correcting for zero errors, better experimental technique.

Cannot be reduced by: Repeating and averaging (repeats give the same wrong value).

🎲 Random Errors

Errors that cause readings to scatter unpredictably above and below the true value. They affect precision.

  • Reaction time when using a stopwatch
  • Estimating between scale divisions
  • Fluctuations — air currents, vibrations
  • Variations in the quantity being measured

Reduced by: Repeating measurements and taking the average, using more sensitive instruments, timing many oscillations.

🎯 Accuracy vs Precision

🎯

Accurate & Precise

Readings are close to the true value AND close to each other. This is the goal!

🎯

Precise but NOT Accurate

Readings are close to each other but consistently off from the true value. Indicates a systematic error.

🎯

Accurate but NOT Precise

Readings average to the true value but are widely scattered. Indicates large random errors.

🎯

Neither Accurate nor Precise

Readings are scattered AND off from the true value. Both error types present.

📐 Calculating Percentage Error

Percentage Error % error = |experimental − accepted| / accepted × 100%
📝 Example — Percentage Error in g

Q: A pendulum experiment gives g = 10.3 m/s². The accepted value is 9.81 m/s². Find the % error.

1
% error = |10.3 − 9.81| / 9.81 × 100% = 0.49/9.81 × 100% = 5.0%
💡 What's a "good" result?
For O-Level practicals, a percentage error of < 5% is considered good. 5–10% is acceptable. > 10% suggests significant errors that should be investigated.
