⚖️ Turning Effects of Forces

Moments, principle of moments, rotating objects, equilibrium, centre of gravity & classic problems

🔧 Moment of a Force

Momentτ = F × d (unit: N m)

A moment is the turning effect of a force about a pivot. The perpendicular distance from the line of action to the pivot determines the moment.

💡 "Perpendicular distance" means the shortest distance from pivot to the line of action of the force. If force is applied at an angle θ to the lever, only the perpendicular component (F sin θ) creates a moment, or equivalently: τ = F × L × sin θ.

Direction of Moments

  • Clockwise (CW)
  • Anticlockwise (ACW)

🔧 Opening a Door

Handle placed far from hinge → maximum perpendicular distance → large moment with small force.

🔩 Long Spanner

Longer spanner = larger d = less force needed for same moment.

⚖️ Principle of Moments

Principle of MomentsFor rotational equilibrium: ΣCW moments = ΣACW moments
💡 Smart Pivot Choice: Take moments about a point where an unknown force acts — its moment = 0 (d = 0).

Conditions for Complete Equilibrium

  1. Translational: Resultant force = 0
  2. Rotational: Resultant moment about any point = 0
📝 Worked Example 1 — Seesaw

Q: 300 N at 1.5 m left; find position for 500 N on right to balance.

1

ACW = 300 × 1.5 = 450 N m

2

500 × d = 450 → d = 0.90 m

📝 Worked Example 2 — Non-Central Pivot

Q: 10 kg plank (4 m), pivot 1.5 m from left, 5 kg at left end. Find F at right end.

1

ACW: 50 × 1.5 = 75 N m

2

CW: 100 × 0.5 + F × 2.5

3

75 = 50 + 2.5F → F = 10 N

🎯 Centre of Gravity & Stability

Centre of GravityThe point where the entire weight may be considered to act.
🔺

Stable

CG rises when displaced → returns.

🔻

Unstable

CG falls when displaced → topples.

🔧 Force at an Angle — Perpendicular Distance

This is one of the most commonly misunderstood concepts in O-Level Physics. When a force acts at an angle to a lever, only the perpendicular component creates a moment — or equivalently, we use the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force.

Moment with Force at Angle θ τ = F × d⊥ = F × L × sin θ
💡 Two equivalent methods to find the moment:

Method 1 — Use perpendicular distance:
Extend the line of action of F. Drop a perpendicular from the pivot to this line. This distance is d⊥ = L sin θ.
τ = F × d⊥

Method 2 — Use perpendicular component of force:
Resolve F into components: F⊥ = F sin θ (perpendicular to lever) and F∥ = F cos θ (along lever).
τ = F⊥ × L = (F sin θ) × L

Both give the same answer! τ = F × L × sin θ
⚠️ Common Exam Mistake: Students often multiply F × L without considering the angle. If F is not perpendicular to the lever, you must include sin θ. When θ = 90°, sin θ = 1 and the formula simplifies to τ = F × L.

📝 Worked Example — Force at an Angle on a Spanner

Q: A force of 60 N is applied at 30° to the end of a 0.25 m spanner. Calculate the moment about the bolt.

1

Identify: F = 60 N, L = 0.25 m, θ = 30°

2

Method 1 (perpendicular distance):

d⊥ = L sin θ = 0.25 × sin 30° = 0.25 × 0.5 = 0.125 m
τ = F × d⊥ = 60 × 0.125 = 7.5 N m
3

Method 2 (perpendicular component):

F⊥ = F sin θ = 60 × sin 30° = 60 × 0.5 = 30 N
τ = F⊥ × L = 30 × 0.25 = 7.5 N m ✓ Same answer!
📝 Worked Example — Rope on a Trapdoor

Q: A rope is attached to the end of a 1.2 m trapdoor, pulling with 80 N at 45° to the door. Find the moment about the hinge.

1
τ = F × L × sin θ = 80 × 1.2 × sin 45°
= 80 × 1.2 × 0.707 = 67.9 N m
2

Compare: If the rope pulled at 90°, τ = 80 × 1.2 × 1 = 96 N m — that's 41% more effective!
