⚖️ Turning Effects of Forces

Moments, principle of moments, conditions for equilibrium, centre of gravity & classic problems

🔧 Moment of a Force

Moment (Torque) Moment = Force × Perpendicular distance from pivot
In symbols τ = F × d    (unit: N m)

Understanding Moment

A moment (or torque) is the turning effect of a force about a pivot (fulcrum). It depends on two factors:

  • The magnitude of the force — a larger force produces a larger moment.
  • The perpendicular distance from the line of action of the force to the pivot — a greater distance produces a larger moment.
💡 "Perpendicular distance" means the shortest distance from the pivot to the line of action of the force. If the force is not perpendicular to the lever, you must resolve it or use the perpendicular distance — not the length of the lever!

Direction of Moments

Moments can be:

  • Clockwise (CW) — the force tends to rotate the object clockwise about the pivot.
  • Anticlockwise (ACW) — the force tends to rotate the object anticlockwise about the pivot.

When calculating the resultant moment, assign one direction as positive and the other as negative.

🔧 Opening a Door

You push at the handle (far from the hinge) rather than near the hinge. The door handle is placed far from the pivot to maximise the perpendicular distance, producing a larger moment with the same force.

🔩 Using a Long Spanner

A longer spanner makes it easier to turn a tight bolt because the perpendicular distance d is larger, so a smaller force F produces the same moment.

⚖️ Principle of Moments

Principle of Moments For a body in rotational equilibrium:
Sum of CW moments = Sum of ACW moments

When an object is in rotational equilibrium (not rotating, or rotating at constant angular velocity), the total clockwise moment about any point equals the total anticlockwise moment about the same point.

💡 Choice of Pivot: You can take moments about any point. A smart choice of pivot eliminates unknown forces that pass through it (since their perpendicular distance = 0, their moment = 0). This simplifies calculations!

Conditions for Equilibrium (Complete)

For a body to be in complete equilibrium, two conditions must be met:

  1. Translational equilibrium: The resultant force = 0 (no linear acceleration).
  2. Rotational equilibrium: The resultant moment about any point = 0 (no angular acceleration).
📝 Worked Example 1 — Simple Seesaw

Question: A uniform beam of length 4 m and weight 200 N is pivoted at its centre. A 300 N weight is placed 1.5 m from the pivot on the left. Where must a 500 N weight be placed on the right to balance the beam?

1

The beam is uniform and pivoted at its centre, so its own weight acts at the pivot. Moment of beam's weight about the pivot = 0.

2

ACW moment = 300 × 1.5 = 450 N m

3

CW moment = 500 × d

By the Principle of Moments: 500 × d = 450

d = 450 ÷ 500 = 0.90 m from the pivot
📝 Worked Example 2 — Non-Central Pivot with Beam Weight

Question: A uniform plank of mass 10 kg and length 4 m rests on a pivot placed 1.5 m from the left end. A 5 kg mass hangs from the left end. Find the force that must be applied at the right end to balance the plank. (g = 10 m/s²)

1

Weight of plank = 10 × 10 = 100 N, acting at centre (2 m from left end).

Weight of mass = 5 × 10 = 50 N, at 0 m (left end).

Pivot is at 1.5 m from left.

2

ACW moments (about pivot):

50 × 1.5 = 75 N m (hanging mass)
3

CW moments (about pivot):

100 × (2.0 − 1.5) = 100 × 0.5 = 50 N m (beam weight)
F × (4.0 − 1.5) = F × 2.5 (applied force at right end)
4

Apply Principle of Moments: ACW = CW

75 = 50 + 2.5F
2.5F = 25
F = 10 N (downward at right end)

🎯 Centre of Gravity (CG)

Definition The centre of gravity is the point where the entire weight of the object may be considered to act.

Key Facts

  • For a uniform (homogeneous) object, the CG is at its geometric centre.
  • For irregular shapes, the CG can be found experimentally using the plumb line method: suspend the object from different points and draw vertical lines — the CG is where the lines intersect.
  • The CG does not have to be inside the object (e.g. a ring's CG is at its centre, which is empty space).
  • When a uniform beam is pivoted at its centre, its weight produces zero moment about the pivot because the perpendicular distance from its weight to the pivot is zero.

Stability

The position of the CG determines how stable an object is.

🔺

Stable Equilibrium

When displaced, CG rises. Object returns to original position. Wide base, low CG.

🔻

Unstable Equilibrium

When displaced, CG falls. Object topples further. Narrow base, high CG.

⚪

Neutral Equilibrium

When displaced, CG stays at same height. Object stays in new position (e.g. ball on flat surface).

💡 Toppling Condition: An object topples when its vertical line from the CG falls outside the base of support. To increase stability: lower the CG and/or widen the base.

🏎️ Racing Cars

Racing cars have wide wheel bases and very low centres of gravity to maximise stability during high-speed turns.

🚌 Double-Decker Buses

Heavy engines are placed in the lower deck to keep the CG low, preventing toppling when turning.

🔄 Couple

Couple A couple is a pair of equal and opposite forces whose lines of action do not coincide.

A couple produces pure rotation with no translational motion (the resultant force is zero, but the resultant moment is not zero).

Torque of a Couple τ = F × d    (d = perpendicular distance between the two forces)

🚗 Steering Wheel

Your hands apply equal and opposite forces on either side of the wheel. The result is pure rotation — the car turns without the steering column translating.
