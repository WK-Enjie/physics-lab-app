Hooke's Law — Spring Extension Experiment

Aim: To investigate the relationship between force (load) and extension of a spring, and to determine the spring constant k.

Apparatus

Helical spring Retort stand + clamp Metre rule Slotted masses (50g each) Mass hanger Pointer / marker

Theory

Hooke's Law F = k × e

Where F = applied force (N), k = spring constant (N/m), e = extension (m).

The law holds up to the limit of proportionality — beyond this, the spring deforms permanently.

💡 Extension vs Length: Extension = Current length − Original (natural) length. Always measure extension, not total length!

Procedure

1
Set up the spring on the clamp stand. Record the natural length L₀ with no load.
2
Add a 50 g mass (0.5 N). Record the new length. Calculate extension = new length − L₀.
3
Continue adding masses in steps of 50 g (up to 350 g). Record length and extension each time.
4
Plot a graph of Force (y-axis) vs Extension (x-axis).
5
The gradient of the straight-line portion = spring constant k.
Natural length: 20.0 cm
Current length: 20.0 cm
Extension: 0.0 cm
Force: 0.00 N

⚠️ Sources of Error & Precautions

  • Parallax error — read the ruler at eye level, perpendicular to the scale.
  • Spring not vertical — ensure spring hangs freely without touching the ruler.
  • Exceeding elastic limit — do not add too much mass; the spring should return to original length when unloaded.
  • Oscillation — wait for the spring to stop oscillating before reading.
  • Zero error — check that the pointer reads correctly at zero load.

🏆 Extension Challenge

  • Predict what happens if you use a stiffer spring. How does the graph change?
  • What would happen if two identical springs are connected in series? In parallel?
  • Add masses beyond the elastic limit — what do you observe about the extension?

Determining Density of Regular & Irregular Objects

Aim: To determine the density of solid objects using measurements of mass and volume.

Apparatus

Electronic balance Ruler / Vernier calipers Measuring cylinder Water Overflow can Various solid objects

Theory

Density ρ = m / V

Density = mass ÷ volume. Unit: kg/m³ or g/cm³.

💡 Methods for Volume:
Regular objects (cubes, cylinders): Calculate from dimensions (V = lwh, V = πr²h).
Irregular objects: Use displacement method — submerge in measuring cylinder and read the rise in water level.
Select an object and method, then click Measure.

⚠️ Sources of Error

  • Meniscus reading — read at the bottom of the meniscus for water.
  • Trapped air bubbles — tap the measuring cylinder to release bubbles when using displacement.
  • Irregular shapes — cannot use ruler method; must use displacement.
  • Object absorbs water — wood, sponge etc. may absorb water, affecting volume reading.
  • Object too large — if it doesn't fit in the measuring cylinder, use an overflow can.

Verification of the Principle of Moments

Aim: To verify that for a body in equilibrium, the sum of clockwise moments equals the sum of anticlockwise moments about any point.

Apparatus

Metre rule Knife-edge pivot Slotted masses String loops Electronic balance

Procedure

1
Balance the metre rule on the knife-edge. Note the balance point (should be ≈50 cm for a uniform rule).
2
Hang mass m₁ on the left side at distance d₁ from pivot.
3
Adjust the position of mass m₂ on the right side until the rule balances horizontally.
4
Record m₁, d₁, m₂, d₂. Calculate CW and ACW moments. They should be equal.
5
Repeat with different masses and positions for at least 5 trials.

Investigating Speed & Acceleration

Aim: To measure speed and determine acceleration of a trolley on a ramp using a ticker tape timer (or equivalent).

Apparatus

Trolley Ramp / inclined plane Ticker tape timer (50 Hz) Ticker tape Ruler Scissors

Theory

Speed from ticker tape speed = distance between dots / time between dots

At 50 Hz, the timer makes 50 dots per second, so the time interval between consecutive dots = 0.02 s.

A common method: cut the tape into strips of 5 dot-spaces (= 0.10 s each) and arrange them as a bar chart to visualise acceleration.

Acceleration a = (v − u) / t    or    a = Δv / Δt
Set ramp angle and release the trolley to generate ticker tape data.

📊 Ticker Tape Strip Chart

Each strip represents a 5-dot interval (0.10 s). Taller strips = faster speed. Increasing height = acceleration.

📈 Speed-Time Graph

—
Acceleration (m/s²) — from gradient of speed-time graph

⚠️ Sources of Error

  • Friction on tape — ticker tape drags through the timer, adding friction.
  • Dots overlap — at low speed, dots are very close; hard to measure accurately.
  • Ruler precision — measurements to ±0.5 mm affect calculated speeds.
  • Release inconsistency — must release cleanly without pushing.
