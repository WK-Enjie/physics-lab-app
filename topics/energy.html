⚡ Conservation of Energy

Energy stores, GPE ↔ KE, conservation principle, energy transfers & efficiency

🔋 Energy Stores — The New Framework

💡 Important Update (Syllabus 6091): The latest syllabus uses the language of energy stores instead of "forms/types of energy". Energy is described as being stored in various ways, and transferred between stores by specific pathways (mechanical work, heating, radiation, electrical work). This is a more accurate description of what energy actually does.

Old Language vs New Language

❌ Old Language (Avoid)✅ New Language (Use)
"Kinetic energy"Kinetic energy store
"Potential energy"Gravitational potential energy store
"Sound energy"Energy transferred by sound waves (not a store)
"Light energy"Energy transferred by light/radiation (not a store)
"Heat energy"Internal (thermal) energy store
"Energy is converted from one form to another""Energy is transferred from one store to another"
⚠️ Key Distinction: Sound and light are not energy stores — they are transfer pathways. Energy is transferred by sound waves or light/electromagnetic radiation from one store to another. You cannot "store" sound.

The 8 Energy Stores

🏃

Kinetic Energy Store

Energy stored in a moving object. Depends on mass and speed. KE = ½mv².

⬆️

Gravitational Potential Energy Store

Energy stored due to an object's height in a gravitational field. GPE = mgh.

🔥

Internal (Thermal) Energy Store

Total KE and PE of all particles in an object. Increases when temperature rises or state changes.

🧲

Elastic Potential Energy Store

Energy stored in a stretched or compressed object (spring, rubber band). E = ½kx².

⚗️

Chemical Energy Store

Energy stored in chemical bonds (food, fuels, batteries). Released in chemical reactions.

☢️

Nuclear Energy Store

Energy stored in the nucleus of atoms. Released in fission or fusion reactions.

🧲

Magnetic Energy Store

Energy stored when magnets attract or repel. Two repelling magnets pushed together store magnetic energy.

⚡

Electrostatic Energy Store

Energy stored in electric fields between charged objects. E.g., energy stored in a capacitor.

Energy moves between stores through four main pathways:

Old: "GPE is converted to KE as the ball falls."

New: "Energy is transferred from the gravitational potential energy store to the kinetic energy store by the mechanism of gravitational force doing work."

💡 KE depends on v²: If you double the speed, the kinetic energy quadruples (× 4). This is why high-speed collisions are so much more destructive, and why braking distance increases dramatically with speed.

⚖️ Principle of Conservation of Energy

Conservation of Energy Energy cannot be created or destroyed. It can only be transferred from one store to another.

The total energy of a closed system remains constant. When energy seems to "disappear" (e.g. a rolling ball slowing down), it has actually been transferred to the internal (thermal) energy store of the surroundings via friction (heating).

GPE ↔ KE Conversion (No Energy Losses)

In an ideal system with no friction or air resistance:

Conservation: GPE → KE mgh = ½mv²   →   v = √(2gh)
💡 Notice: In the equation v = √(2gh), the speed at the bottom does not depend on mass! A heavy ball and a light ball dropped from the same height reach the same speed (in the absence of air resistance). This is consistent with Galileo's famous experiment.
📝 Worked Example 1 — Object Falling from Height

Question: A 2 kg ball is dropped from a height of 5 m. Calculate its speed just before hitting the ground. Assume no air resistance. (g = 10 m/s²)

1

Identify energy transfer: GPE store → KE store

At the top: all GPE, no KE (v = 0)

At the bottom: all KE, no GPE (h = 0)

2

Apply conservation:

GPE lost = KE gained
mgh = ½mv²
3

Mass cancels (or substitute):

v² = 2gh = 2 × 10 × 5 = 100
v = √100 = 10 m/s
📝 Worked Example 2 — Roller Coaster with Energy Loss

Question: A 500 kg roller coaster car starts from rest at a height of 40 m. At the bottom, its speed is 25 m/s. Calculate the energy transferred to the internal (thermal) energy store of the surroundings. (g = 10 m/s²)

1
GPE at top = mgh = 500 × 10 × 40 = 200 000 J
2
KE at bottom = ½mv² = ½ × 500 × 25² = 156 250 J
3

Energy transferred to internal (thermal) store:

E_thermal = GPE − KE = 200 000 − 156 250 = 43 750 J

📊 Efficiency

Efficiency Efficiency = (Useful energy output / Total energy input) × 100%
Also expressed as Efficiency = (Useful power output / Total power input) × 100%
  • Efficiency is a ratio — it has no units (or expressed as a percentage).
  • Efficiency is always less than 100% in real systems because some energy is always transferred to the internal (thermal) energy store of the surroundings ("wasted" as heat due to friction, air resistance, etc.).
  • A machine does not create energy — it only transfers it. A more efficient machine wastes less energy.
⚠️ Common Mistake: Efficiency can never exceed 100%. If your calculation gives more than 100%, check your working — you may have swapped input and output!
📝 Worked Example — Efficiency of a Motor

Question: An electric motor uses 800 J of electrical energy to lift a 5 kg mass through 12 m. Calculate the efficiency. (g = 10 m/s²)

1

Useful energy output = GPE gained = mgh = 5 × 10 × 12 = 600 J

2
Efficiency = (600 / 800) × 100% = 75%
3

The remaining 200 J (25%) was transferred to the internal (thermal) energy store of the motor and surroundings due to friction and electrical resistance.
