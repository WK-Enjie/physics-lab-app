🚀 Newton's Laws of Motion

Inertia, F = ma, action-reaction pairs, free body diagrams, connected objects & types of forces

⚖️ Newton's First Law — The Law of Inertia

Newton's First Law Every body continues in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line unless acted upon by a resultant external force.

What Is Inertia?

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist any change in its state of motion. It is not a force — it is a property of matter.

💡 Key Idea: The mass of an object is a measure of its inertia. A more massive object has greater inertia — it is harder to start moving, harder to stop, and harder to change direction.

Understanding the First Law

  • If the resultant force = 0, the object is either at rest or moving at constant velocity (constant speed in a straight line).
  • An object does not need a force to keep moving — only to change its motion.
  • "Resultant force" means the vector sum of all forces. Individual forces may exist, but if they cancel out, the result is the same as no force.

Everyday Examples

🚗 Seatbelts

When a car stops suddenly, your body tends to continue moving forward (inertia). The seatbelt provides the external force to decelerate your body with the car.

🪙 Coin on Card Trick

Place a coin on a card on top of a glass. Flick the card away quickly — the coin drops into the glass. The coin's inertia keeps it roughly stationary while the card is swept away.

🧹 Hitting a Dusty Carpet

When you beat a carpet, the carpet suddenly moves forward but the dust stays behind due to inertia, separating from the carpet.

🔥 Newton's Second Law — F = ma

Newton's Second Law Fnet = m × a

The resultant (net) force acting on an object equals the product of its mass and acceleration.

QuantitySymbolSI UnitNotes
Resultant ForceFNewton (N)Vector — has direction
Massmkilogram (kg)Scalar — always positive
Accelerationam/s²Vector — same direction as F
💡 Definition of 1 Newton: 1 N is the resultant force needed to give a 1 kg mass an acceleration of 1 m/s².

Weight — A Special Case of F = ma

Weight W = m × g     (g = 10 m/s² for O-Levels)

Weight is the gravitational force on an object. Since gravity causes a free-falling object to accelerate at g, applying F = ma gives W = mg.

⚠️ Common Mistake: Mass and weight are not the same. Mass (kg) is a measure of matter; weight (N) is a gravitational force. A 60 kg person has a weight of 600 N on Earth, but only ~100 N on the Moon.
📝 Worked Example 1 — Horizontal Force

Question: A 4 kg block on a smooth (frictionless) surface is pushed by a horizontal force of 20 N. Find the acceleration.

1

Identify: F = 20 N, m = 4 kg, surface is smooth → no friction.

2

Apply Newton's Second Law:

F = ma
20 = 4 × a
3

Solve for acceleration:

a = 20 ÷ 4 = 5.0 m/s²
📝 Worked Example 2 — With Friction

Question: A 5 kg box is pushed by a 40 N horizontal force on a rough surface. Friction is 15 N. Find the acceleration.

1

Identify forces in the horizontal direction:

Applied force = 40 N (→)    Friction = 15 N (←)

2

Find resultant (net) force:

Fnet = 40 − 15 = 25 N (→)
3

Apply F = ma:

25 = 5 × a
a = 25 ÷ 5 = 5.0 m/s² (→)

🔄 Newton's Third Law — Action & Reaction

Newton's Third Law If body A exerts a force on body B, then body B exerts an equal and opposite force on body A.

Properties of Action–Reaction Pairs

PropertyDescription
Equal in magnitudeBoth forces are the same size
Opposite in directionThey point in opposite directions
Act on different bodiesOne force on body A, the other on body B
Same type of forceBoth are the same kind (e.g. both gravitational, both contact)
Exist simultaneouslyThey appear and disappear together
⚠️ Action–Reaction ≠ Balanced Forces!
Balanced forces act on the same object. Action–reaction pairs act on different objects. A book resting on a table: its weight and the normal force are balanced forces (both act on the book). The action–reaction pair for the book's weight is the gravitational pull of the book on the Earth.

Examples

🚶 Walking

Action: Your foot pushes backward on the ground (friction).
Reaction: The ground pushes your foot forward (friction).
Both forces are frictional forces and act on different bodies (foot vs ground).

🚀 Rocket Propulsion

Action: Rocket pushes exhaust gases downward.
Reaction: Exhaust gases push rocket upward.
This is why rockets work in space — no air needed!

🏷️ Types of Forces

In O-Level Physics, you need to identify and describe several types of forces. Each has specific properties and directions.

W

Weight (Gravitational Force)

W = mg. Acts vertically downward from centre of gravity toward centre of Earth. Acts on all objects with mass, even in mid-air.

N

Normal Contact Force

Acts perpendicular to the surface of contact. A "push-back" force from a surface. Only exists when objects are in contact.

f

Friction

Acts parallel to the surface, opposing motion (or attempted motion). Depends on surface roughness and normal force. f ≤ μN.

T

Tension

A pulling force along a string, rope, or cable. Acts away from the object along the line of the string. For a massless string, tension is the same throughout.

D

Air Resistance / Drag

Acts opposite to velocity (against motion through a fluid). Increases with speed and surface area. At terminal velocity, drag = weight.

F

Applied / Push / Pull Force

Any externally applied force. Direction is specified in the problem. Can act at any angle.

U

Upthrust (Buoyancy)

Upward force on an object in a fluid. Equals the weight of fluid displaced. Acts through centre of buoyancy.

S

Spring / Elastic Force

Restoring force in a stretched or compressed spring. Acts to return the spring to its natural length. F = kx (Hooke's Law).

🔗 Connected Objects — Same Acceleration!

When two objects are connected by an inextensible (inelastic) string, they must have the same acceleration. The string also has the same tension throughout (if massless).

💡 Problem-Solving Strategy:
Step 1: Treat the whole system as one object to find acceleration.
Step 2: Isolate one object and apply F = ma to find tension.
⚠️ Inelastic string does not mean the string stores energy. It means the string does not stretch, so both objects have the same speed and acceleration at all times.
📝 Worked Example — Connected Objects over a Pulley

Question: A block of mass 4 kg on a smooth table is connected by a light string over a smooth pulley to a hanging block of mass 6 kg. Find (a) the acceleration and (b) the tension in the string. (g = 10 m/s²)

1

System approach: The only unbalanced external force driving the system is the weight of m₂.

Fnet = m₂g = 6 × 10 = 60 N
Total mass = m₁ + m₂ = 4 + 6 = 10 kg
2

(a) Find acceleration:

a = Fnet / (m₁ + m₂) = 60 / 10 = 6.0 m/s²
3

(b) Find tension: Isolate m₁ on the table. The only horizontal force is T.

T = m₁ × a = 4 × 6 = 24 N
✓

Check: Isolate m₂. Net downward force = m₂g − T = 60 − 24 = 36 N.

a = 36 / 6 = 6.0 m/s² ✓ Same acceleration!
