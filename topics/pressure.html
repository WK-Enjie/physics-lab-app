💧 Pressure

P = F/A, liquid pressure P = hρg, hydraulic systems, Pascal's principle & manometers

📏 Pressure — P = F / A

Pressure P = F / A

Pressure is defined as the force acting per unit area.

QuantitySymbolSI UnitNotes
PressurePPascal (Pa)1 Pa = 1 N/m²
ForceFNewton (N)Perpendicular to surface
AreaAm²Contact area
💡 Key Insight: For the same force, a smaller area gives a higher pressure. This is why sharp objects (small area) exert large pressures, and why snowshoes (large area) reduce the pressure on snow.

Everyday Applications

🔪 Sharp Knife

A sharp blade has a very small contact area, so the same cutting force produces a very high pressure, slicing through food easily.

🎿 Snowshoes / Skis

Wide snowshoes spread your weight over a large area, greatly reducing the pressure on snow so you don't sink in.

📌 Drawing Pin

The sharp point (tiny area) exerts very high pressure to pierce the board, while the flat head (large area) has low pressure so it doesn't hurt your thumb.

🐪 Camel's Feet

Camels have wide, padded feet to spread their weight over a larger area, reducing pressure on the sand so they don't sink.

📝 Worked Example — Brick on a Table

Question: A brick weighing 30 N has dimensions 20 cm × 10 cm × 5 cm. Calculate the maximum and minimum pressure it can exert on a surface.

1

Maximum pressure → smallest face in contact.

Smallest face: 10 cm × 5 cm = 50 cm² = 0.0050 m²

P_max = 30 / 0.0050 = 6000 Pa
2

Minimum pressure → largest face in contact.

Largest face: 20 cm × 10 cm = 200 cm² = 0.0200 m²

P_min = 30 / 0.0200 = 1500 Pa

🌊 Pressure in Liquids — P = hρg

Liquid Pressure P = h × ρ × g
QuantitySymbolSI UnitNotes
PressurePPaAt a given depth
DepthhmMeasured vertically from surface
Density of liquidρkg/m³ρ_water = 1000 kg/m³
Gravitational field strengthgN/kg (m/s²)g = 10 m/s²

Key Properties of Liquid Pressure

  • Liquid pressure increases with depth — the deeper you go, the greater the weight of liquid above you.
  • Liquid pressure acts equally in all directions at a given depth.
  • Liquid pressure does NOT depend on the shape or volume of the container — only on depth, density and g.
  • At the same depth, a denser liquid produces higher pressure.
  • The total pressure at depth h = atmospheric pressure + hρg.
💡 Container Shape Independence: Water at 3 m depth has the same pressure whether it's in a narrow tube, a wide lake, or an oddly-shaped flask. This is sometimes called the hydrostatic paradox.

📝 Worked Example — Liquid Pressure at Depth

Question: Find the pressure due to the water at the bottom of a swimming pool 2.5 m deep. (ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 10 m/s²)

1

Identify: h = 2.5 m, ρ = 1000 kg/m³, g = 10 m/s²

2
P = hρg = 2.5 × 1000 × 10 = 25 000 Pa = 25 kPa
3

Note: This is the pressure due to the water only. The total (absolute) pressure includes atmospheric pressure:

P_total = P_atm + hρg ≈ 100 000 + 25 000 = 125 000 Pa

The Manometer

A manometer is a U-tube containing a liquid (often mercury or water) used to measure gas pressure. The difference in liquid levels Δh gives the excess pressure of the gas above atmospheric pressure.

Manometer Reading P_gas = P_atm + Δh × ρ × g    (if gas side is higher)
P_gas = P_atm − Δh × ρ × g    (if gas side is lower)

⚙️ Hydraulic Press & Pascal's Principle

Pascal's Principle A pressure change applied to an enclosed fluid is transmitted undiminished to every part of the fluid and to the walls of the container.

A hydraulic press uses Pascal's principle to multiply force. A small force on a small piston creates a pressure that is transmitted to a large piston, producing a large force.

Hydraulic Press Formula P₁ = P₂ → F₁/A₁ = F₂/A₂
Force Multiplication F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁)
💡 Force Multiplication ≠ Energy Creation: While the output force is larger, the output piston moves a shorter distance. Work in = Work out (in an ideal system). The hydraulic press is a force multiplier, not an energy creator!
Volume Conservation (Incompressible Fluid) A₁ × d₁ = A₂ × d₂

Properties of Good Hydraulic Fluids

💧

Incompressible

Liquids cannot be compressed significantly, so pressure is transmitted instantly and completely. (Gases would compress, absorbing force.)

🌡️

High Boiling Point

Prevents the fluid from vaporising at high temperatures generated during operation. Vapour bubbles would be compressible.

❄️

Low Freezing Point

Allows the system to operate in cold conditions without the fluid solidifying and blocking the system.

🛡️

Non-Corrosive

Must not corrode or damage the seals, pipes, and pistons of the hydraulic system, ensuring long service life.

🔥

Non-Flammable

Safety requirement — hydraulic fluid should not catch fire, especially in machinery and vehicles where heat is generated.

🫧

Low Viscosity

Fluid must flow easily through pipes and around pistons. High viscosity would slow the system and waste energy as heat.

⚠️ Why Not Use Gas? Gases are compressible. If air gets into a hydraulic brake system, pressing the brake pedal compresses the air bubble instead of transmitting force to the brakes. This is called "spongy brakes" — very dangerous! The liquid must be kept free of air bubbles.

Hydraulic Applications

🚗 Hydraulic Brakes

Foot presses small piston → pressure transmits through brake fluid → large pistons push brake pads against disc. Small input force, large braking force.

🏗️ Hydraulic Lift / Jack

A small hand-pumped piston transmits pressure to a large piston that lifts heavy vehicles or machinery. Force multiplied by area ratio.

💺 Dentist's Chair

Hydraulic system raises/lowers the chair smoothly with minimal effort by the operator, using the force multiplication principle.

📝 Worked Example — Hydraulic Press

Question: A hydraulic press has a small piston of area 0.02 m² and a large piston of area 0.50 m². A force of 100 N is applied to the small piston. Find: (a) the pressure transmitted, (b) the force on the large piston, (c) the distance moved by the large piston if the small piston moves 0.3 m.

1

(a) Pressure transmitted:

P = F₁ / A₁ = 100 / 0.02 = 5000 Pa
2

(b) Force on large piston:

F₂ = P × A₂ = 5000 × 0.50 = 2500 N

Or: F₂ = F₁ × (A₂/A₁) = 100 × (0.50/0.02) = 100 × 25 = 2500 N

3

(c) Distance moved by large piston (volume conserved):

A₁d₁ = A₂d₂
0.02 × 0.3 = 0.50 × d₂
d₂ = 0.006 / 0.50 = 0.012 m = 1.2 cm
✓

Check energy conservation:

Work in = F₁ × d₁ = 100 × 0.3 = 30 J
Work out = F₂ × d₂ = 2500 × 0.012 = 30 J ✓
